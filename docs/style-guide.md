# Pulse & Heritage — Writing Style Guide

*Because everyone deserves to be seen, heard, and treated appropriately.*

This guide defines the editorial voice for Pulse & Heritage. It applies to every published piece — articles, condition pages, story features, social copy, and email.

---

## Who writes here

Pulse & Heritage is authored by **Allison Smith, MSN, FNP-C** — a board-certified Family Nurse Practitioner with ICU, cardiac, and oncology clinical experience, a VA clinic rotation, and 8 years in the U.S. Armed Forces (two tours in Iraq).

Three identities sit behind every sentence. Writing must hold all three at once:

1. **The Clinician** — board-certified NP, ICU/cardiac/oncology
2. **The Veteran** — 8 years, 2 tours Iraq, direct
3. **The Advocate** — person of color, has seen the system fail people who look like her

If a piece reads like only one of those voices, it isn't finished.

---

## The core principle

**Clinically precise, universally clear.**

This is the Stephen Hawking model: accessible is *not* the same as simplified. Use the real clinical term, then make it real.

> "Your A1C — a 3-month average of your blood sugar — was 7.2."

Not:

> "Your blood sugar number was a little high."

The reader is intelligent at every reading level. Trust them.

---

## Audience

Veterans and people of color across the Black diaspora — African American, Caribbean, African, Afro-Latino. Writing speaks **to** the reader (*your, our*), never **about** them (*those communities, that population*).

---

## Do

- **Use the clinical term, then make it real.** Name the condition, the lab, the medication, the guideline — then translate.
- **Build bridges with analogies.** Familiar comparisons unlock unfamiliar concepts.
- **Name systemic problems directly.** Race, bias, service-related inequity — by name. No euphemisms.
- **Speak to the reader.** *Your kidneys, our community, you can ask.*
- **Cite the study, name the guideline, link the source.** Specific journal, specific year, specific recommendation.
- **Show controlled anger at broken systems.** Not at people. At pain bias, at the eGFR race coefficient, at VA claim denials.
- **End every piece with what the reader can do.** A question to ask, a form to file, a number to track, a script to use.
- **Trust the reader's intelligence at every reading level.**

## Don't

- **Don't hide behind jargon.** If you wouldn't say it out loud to a patient, don't write it.
- **Don't strip clinical terms entirely.** Removing the real word leaves the reader unable to advocate for themselves.
- **Don't dance around race or bias.** Vague language protects the system, not the reader.
- **Don't talk about "those communities" from outside.** The author and the reader are inside the same room.
- **Don't make vague claims about "disparities" without specifics.** Name the gap. Cite the number. Identify the cause.
- **Don't lecture or moralize.** The reader knows disparities are bad. Skip the sermon.
- **Don't be hopeless or fatalistic.** Honest about systemic failure, but every piece points to agency.
- **Don't leave the reader feeling powerless.**
- **Don't condescend or oversimplify.**

---

## Tone guardrails

- **Never preachy.** The reader doesn't need a lecture on why disparities are bad.
- **Never hopeless.** Honest about systemic failure, but every piece points to agency.
- **Never academic.** Cite the journal, then translate it.
- **Never performative.** No virtue signaling. The work is the point.
- **Never generic.** If it could appear word-for-word on WebMD, it isn't Pulse & Heritage.

## Structural defaults

- **Lead with the human stake**, not the epidemiology. The number lands harder *after* the reader knows who it's about.
- **Define on first use.** Every clinical term gets a plain-language gloss the first time it appears.
- **Cite inline.** Source, year, and a link. Not "studies show."
- **Close with action.** A specific next step the reader can take this week.

## Anti-patterns (AI tells to scrub)

These are the patterns AI-generated prose leans on. They are not Allison's voice. Sweep every draft for them before publishing.

**Punctuation**

- **Em-dash overuse.** The em dash is the single biggest tell of AI-drafted prose. Default to a period (Allison's voice is staccato-declarative — periods land harder), then a colon (when introducing a list or elaboration), then a comma. Em dashes are fine in moderation: byline attributions, the rare dramatic aside. If a page has more than one or two, you've over-used.
- **Double-em-dash parentheticals.** *"In high-acuity care — ICU, oncology, cardiac procedures — and in a VA clinic..."* Restructure with commas or split into sentences.

**Constructions**

- **"Not just X, but Y."** *"Not just medical conditions, but the systems behind them."* Make the point directly. Both halves are true; just say so.
- **"It's not about A — it's about B."** Same family. State B without dismissing A.
- **Forced triples.** *"Faster, smarter, better."* Three for the rhythm, not the meaning. Cut to what's true.
- **Transition word overload.** *"Additionally... Furthermore... Moreover..."* Pick none. Let the next sentence carry itself.
- **Enthusiastic openers.** *"Great news!" "Exciting update!"* Nothing is exciting; something is true.
- **Cliché closers.** *"At the end of the day," "Rest assured," "Without further ado."*

**Vocabulary (LLM fingerprints)**

- *delve, navigate the complexities, in the realm of*
- *unlock the potential, harness the power of*
- *leverage, synergy, robust, seamless, transformative, innovative, world-class, cutting-edge, best-in-class*
- *myriad of, tapestry, landscape* (as metaphor), *journey* (non-travel)
- *empower, elevate, optimize, streamline*
- *In today's fast-paced world, It's important to note that, First and foremost*

**Hedging**

- *might, perhaps, could potentially, may help, can sometimes.* If you don't know, say you don't know. If you do know, say it.

## Quick self-check before publishing

- Does it use the real clinical term *and* translate it?
- Does it name the systemic issue by name?
- Does it speak *to* the reader, not *about* them?
- Is at least one source cited specifically?
- Does the reader leave with something they can do?
- Could this appear word-for-word on WebMD? (If yes, rewrite.)
- Did you sweep for AI tells (see anti-patterns above)?
